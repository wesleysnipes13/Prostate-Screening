Anatomy 101

What Is the Prostate?

The prostate is a small gland — roughly the size of a walnut — located just below the bladder and in front of the rectum in men. It surrounds the urethra (the tube that carries urine and semen out of the body).

The prostate's primary function is to produce seminal fluid, the liquid component of semen that nourishes and helps transport sperm. It plays an important role in male fertility and sexual function, but is not essential for urination (though its location means prostate disease often affects urinary flow).

Anatomical position of the prostate relative to the bladder, urethra, and rectum

As men age, the prostate naturally grows larger — a non-cancerous condition called Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH). BPH affects more than half of men over age 60 and is one of the most common reasons for an elevated PSA level.

💡 Key Takeaway

Because the prostate surrounds the urethra, any condition that enlarges or inflames it can affect urinary flow. Not all prostate problems are cancer — in fact, most prostate-related symptoms in men over 50 are due to benign conditions.

The Disease

What Is Prostate Cancer?

Prostate cancer occurs when cells in the prostate gland grow out of control. Like all cancers, it begins when the DNA inside a normal prostate cell undergoes mutations that cause it to divide uncontrollably.

Prostate cancer is the most common non-skin cancer in American men, with approximately 300,000 new cases diagnosed each year. However, the vast majority of prostate cancers are slow-growing and may not become life-threatening. This is why screening decisions require careful shared decision-making — not every detected cancer will require treatment.

🐢 Slow-Growing (Low Grade)

Many prostate cancers grow very slowly — taking years or even decades to become a clinical problem. These may be managed with active surveillance (careful monitoring without immediate treatment).

⚡ Aggressive (High Grade)

A minority of prostate cancers are aggressive and can spread to lymph nodes, bones, and other organs (metastasize) if not treated. Early detection through PSA screening can identify these before they spread.

How Does Prostate Cancer Develop?

Prostate cancer most often begins in the outer (peripheral) zone of the gland and typically starts as a microscopic focus of abnormal cells. Over time, without intervention, it can grow and potentially break through the prostate capsule (the outer wall of the gland), spread to nearby lymph nodes, and eventually metastasize to distant organs — most commonly bone.

Warning Signs

Symptoms of Prostate Cancer

⚠️ A Critical Point About Symptoms

Early-stage prostate cancer typically causes no symptoms at all. This is exactly why PSA screening exists — to detect cancer before it becomes symptomatic. By the time symptoms appear, the disease may be more advanced.

When symptoms do occur, they often overlap significantly with symptoms of Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH), making it impossible to distinguish cancer from a benign enlargement based on symptoms alone.

Urinary Symptoms

  • Frequent urination, especially at night (nocturia)
  • Difficulty starting urination (hesitancy)
  • Weak or interrupted urine stream
  • Feeling that the bladder doesn't fully empty
  • Pain or burning during urination (more common with prostatitis)
  • Blood in the urine (hematuria) — always warrants evaluation

Sexual & Other Symptoms

  • Blood in semen (hematospermia)
  • Painful ejaculation
  • Erectile dysfunction (can have many causes)

Symptoms of Advanced Disease

  • Bone pain (especially in the lower back, hips, or pelvis) — can indicate metastases
  • Unexplained weight loss and fatigue
  • Leg weakness or swelling (due to lymph node involvement)
🔴 When to See a Doctor Promptly

Blood in the urine or semen, or new bone pain (especially in the back or hips) in a man over 50, should prompt a prompt medical evaluation. These symptoms are not always cancer — but they always warrant investigation.

Detection

Screening & Diagnosis

Prostate cancer screening involves testing men who have no symptoms. The two primary screening tools are:

🩸 PSA Blood Test

A simple blood test measuring the level of Prostate-Specific Antigen (PSA) protein. PSA is produced by prostate cells — both normal and cancerous. An elevated PSA triggers further evaluation but does not confirm cancer.

  • Quick and minimally invasive
  • Can detect cancer before symptoms develop
  • Can be elevated by many non-cancerous conditions (BPH, prostatitis, recent activity)

🖐️ Digital Rectal Exam (DRE)

A physical examination where the physician feels the back surface of the prostate through the rectal wall. Takes about 10–15 seconds.

  • Can detect a hard nodule or irregular texture even if PSA is normal
  • An abnormal DRE alone is an indication for further evaluation
  • Recommended when PSA ≥2.0 ng/mL per current guidelines

The Diagnostic Pathway

Screening detects a signal. Diagnosis requires additional steps:

Step 1: PSA Screening + Optional DRE
Elevated PSA → Confirm with repeat test (wait a few weeks)
↓
Step 2: Confound Exclusion + Secondary Biomarkers
PSA Density, %fPSA, PHI, 4Kscore — helps distinguish BPH from cancer
↓
Step 3: Multiparametric MRI (mpMRI)
Imaging of the prostate — reported using PI-RADS scoring (1–5)
↓
Step 4: Prostate Biopsy (if indicated)
Tissue samples taken for laboratory analysis — the only way to definitively diagnose prostate cancer
Cancer Classification

How Is Prostate Cancer Graded?

If cancer is found on biopsy, it is graded to determine how aggressive it appears under the microscope. Two systems are used together:

Gleason Score

A pathologist examines the biopsy tissue and assigns a Gleason grade (1–5) to the two most common patterns of cancer cells seen. These two grades are added to produce the Gleason Score (ranging from 6 to 10).

Grade Groups (Modern System)

Grade Group Gleason Score Risk Level What It Means
Grade Group 1 Gleason 6 (3+3) Very Low / Low Slowest-growing. Often suitable for active surveillance without immediate treatment.
Grade Group 2 Gleason 7 (3+4) Low-Intermediate Predominantly slow-growing pattern. Treatment decisions individualized.
Grade Group 3 Gleason 7 (4+3) High-Intermediate More aggressive pattern predominates. Treatment generally recommended.
Grade Group 4 Gleason 8 (4+4) High Aggressive cancer. Treatment strongly recommended.
Grade Group 5 Gleason 9–10 Very High Most aggressive. Highest risk of spread. Prompt treatment typically required.
✅ Why Grade Group Matters for Screening

PSA screening is most valuable for detecting Grade Group ≥2 cancers — those with clinically significant potential. Grade Group 1 cancers (Gleason 6) almost never spread and may not require treatment. Modern secondary biomarker tests (like PHI and 4Kscore) are specifically designed to help identify Grade Group ≥2 cancers.

Treatment Overview

How Is Prostate Cancer Treated?

Treatment decisions depend on the cancer's Grade Group, PSA level, imaging findings, patient age, overall health, and personal preferences. Prostate cancer treatment is highly individualized.

For whom: Typically for Grade Group 1 (Gleason 6) and selected Grade Group 2 low-risk cancers.

What it involves: Regular PSA testing (typically every 3–6 months), repeat biopsies every 1–3 years, and periodic MRI imaging. If the cancer shows signs of progression, treatment can then begin.

Goal: Avoid the side effects of treatment while catching any progression early. Research shows that many low-grade cancers never progress during a man's lifetime.

Active surveillance is not the same as "doing nothing." It is an active, guideline-endorsed management strategy with regular monitoring checkpoints.

What it involves: Surgical removal of the entire prostate gland and seminal vesicles, often with lymph node sampling. Increasingly performed as a robotic-assisted (da Vinci) minimally invasive procedure.

Benefits: Removes the cancer entirely; PSA should drop to near-zero after surgery; allows precise pathological staging.

Potential side effects: Urinary incontinence (temporary in most, but can be longer-lasting), erectile dysfunction (risk varies by nerve-sparing technique and age), and the general risks of surgery. Most men regain urinary control within weeks to months.

Recovery: Typically 1–3 days in hospital; return to light activity in 2–4 weeks; full recovery 4–6 weeks.

There are two primary forms of radiation therapy for prostate cancer:

External Beam Radiation Therapy (EBRT): High-energy X-ray beams are directed at the prostate from outside the body. Modern IMRT (Intensity-Modulated Radiation Therapy) and SBRT (Stereotactic Body Radiation Therapy) allow very precise targeting with fewer treatment sessions.

Brachytherapy (Seed Implants): Radioactive seeds are placed directly into the prostate tissue, delivering radiation from inside. Can be combined with external beam radiation for higher-risk disease.

Potential side effects: Bowel changes (diarrhea, rectal irritation), urinary changes, and erectile dysfunction. These are generally less severe than with surgery in the short term, but some effects may develop over years.

Prostate cancer cells use testosterone (androgen) to grow. Hormone therapy reduces testosterone levels in the body, slowing or stopping cancer growth.

Forms: Injections (Lupron, Zoladex), oral medications (enzalutamide, apalutamide, abiraterone), or surgical removal of the testes (rarely done today).

Used for: High-risk localized cancer (combined with radiation), locally advanced cancer, metastatic disease, or when cancer returns after initial treatment.

Side effects: Hot flashes, fatigue, loss of libido, erectile dysfunction, mood changes, bone density loss, metabolic effects. Regular monitoring and supportive care can help manage these.

For selected patients with localized, low-to-intermediate risk cancer in one region of the prostate, focal therapies aim to treat only the affected area while sparing the rest of the gland — potentially reducing side effects.

Examples: High-Intensity Focused Ultrasound (HIFU), cryotherapy, MRI-guided focused ultrasound (MRgFUS), photodynamic therapy.

Current status: Still considered investigational at many centers. Evidence base is growing but long-term data are less mature than for surgery or radiation. Best discussed with an experienced urologist or radiation oncologist in a center that offers these options.

For metastatic or hormone-resistant (castration-resistant) prostate cancer, additional therapies may be used:

  • Chemotherapy: Docetaxel and cabazitaxel are used in metastatic disease
  • PARP Inhibitors: Olaparib, rucaparib — effective in men with BRCA or other DNA repair mutations
  • Lutetium-177 PSMA Therapy: A targeted radioligand therapy approved for PSMA-positive metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer
  • Immunotherapy: Sipuleucel-T (Provenge) — a personalized immune therapy for asymptomatic or minimally symptomatic metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer
Procedure Explained

What Is a Digital Rectal Exam (DRE)?

The Digital Rectal Exam (DRE) is a brief physical examination in which a physician — wearing a gloved, lubricated finger — gently inserts a finger into the rectum to feel the back surface of the prostate. The word "digital" refers to the finger, not a computer.

What the doctor feels for: The normal prostate feels smooth and rubbery. An abnormal DRE might reveal a hard nodule, firm area, or asymmetry — which can independently indicate the need for further evaluation, regardless of the PSA level.

Duration: The exam itself takes approximately 10–15 seconds.

Discomfort level: Most men describe it as uncomfortable but not painful. It is a brief and important part of prostate cancer screening, particularly when PSA is ≥2.0 ng/mL.

📋 Guideline Note

Per AUA/SUO 2023/2026 guidelines, DRE is recommended alongside PSA when PSA is ≥2.0 ng/mL. Importantly, PSA blood should ideally be drawn before a DRE, as the exam can cause a transient elevation in PSA levels.
